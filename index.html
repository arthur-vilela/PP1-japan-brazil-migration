Japanese-Brazilian migration

A historical overview of a long distance connection

History

Ryo Mizuno (center), who organized the first trip of Japanese immigrants to Brazil

Necessity for imigration in Japan

At the beginning of the 20th century, Japan faced overpopulation and agricultural difficulties after 265 years of isolation during the Edo period. The modernization and agrarian reform of the Meiji era, despite advances, caused unemployment and indebtedness among peasants, forcing many to migrate to already saturated cities. To ease social tensions and facilitate modernization, Japanese government encouraged emigration, mainly to the United States, Hawaii, Peru and Brazil. Minister Fukashi Sugimura's visit to Brazil in 1905, and his positive report on Brazil's receptiveness, intensified Japanese interest in Brazil, leading many to emigrate individually.

Japanese poster from 1920 incentivizing imigration to Brazil and Peru

Necessity for imigration in Brazil

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Brazil's coffee economy demanded cheap labor, prompting a policy of immigration. The first official attempt to establish a diplomatic and commercial agreement with Japan took place in 1880, culminating in the signing of the Treaty of Friendship, Commerce and Navigation in 1895, which paved the way for Japanese immigration. Despite this, there was strong prejudice against Asian immigrants, seen as inferior races who would threaten the "whitening" of Brazil promoted by European immigration. Fears about the "yellow peril" and Japanese expansionism increased resistance, but the need for workers overcame these barriers. Ryo Mizuno, president of the Imperial Emigration Company, visited Brazil in 1906, and the contract to bring 3,000 Japanese immigrants was signed in 1907, despite criticism. The São Paulo government saw the Japanese as an alternative to fill the gap left by the Italians and solve labor conflicts, taking advantage of the Japanese workforce valued by positive reports of their performance in Hawaii.

Timeline of Events

1908

First Japanese Immigrants Arrive

The Kasato Maru is recognized as the first ship to bring Japanese immigrants to Brazil, disembarking 781 people at the Port of Santos on June 18, 1908. After medical examinations and Portuguese lessons, these immigrants went to work in the coffee plantations of São Paulo. The reception was cold, with little praise, and many immigrants, unaccustomed to farming, abandoned their jobs. In 1910, the Ryojun Maru brought another 906 immigrants, bringing the number of Japanese in Brazil to 10,000 in 1914. The immigrants struggled to adapt, but through the "partnership farming" system, they managed to save money and buy land. Many decided to stay in Brazil, forming communities such as the Liberdade neighborhood in São Paulo.

The first Japanese immigrants arrived in Brazil on the Kasato Maru ship.

1920s

Growth of Japanese Communities

After the First World War, the flow of Japanese immigrants to Brazil increased significantly, with 164,000 arriving between 1917 and 1940, especially in the 1920s. From 1930 onwards, the kôtakusei stood out, immigrants prepared by the Kokushikan Kôtô Takushoku Gakkô in Tokyo to settle permanently in Brazil, not only to work, but also to do research. Immigration to Brazil was stimulated by the United States' ban on the entry of Japanese in 1924 and the restrictions imposed by other countries such as Australia and Canada, as well as Japanese advertisements for getting rich quick in Brazil. As a result, Brazil became one of the few countries to accept Japanese immigrants, facilitating the growth of this community.

Japanese communities began to grow in São Paulo and other regions.

1930s

Restrictions and Challenges

Japanese immigration to Brazil grew during the 1930s, with 75% of them settling in São Paulo to work in coffee plantations, as well as other crops such as strawberries, tea and rice. Small communities also sprang up in Pará, attracted by the cultivation of black pepper. In the 1930s, Brazil was already home to the largest population of Japanese outside Japan. In 1933, the National Constituent Assembly discussed the theses of racial eugenics defended by doctors such as Miguel Couto, who proposed the "whitening" of the Brazilian population and an end to Japanese immigration. In the 1934 census, the Japanese were the third nationality in terms of the number of agricultural establishments in São Paulo, behind Brazilians and Italians.

The resulting Constitution established immigration quotas, allowing only 2% of the total number of immigrants received in the last 50 years, except for the Portuguese. These quotas restricted Japanese immigration and future immigration of Koreans and Chinese. Japanese immigration was the subject of intense debate, with nationalist and racist arguments against their entry, while defenders pointed to the agricultural productivity of the Japanese. During Getúlio Vargas' Estado Novo, repression of immigrant culture intensified, with confiscation of property and bans on political activities, associations and the use of foreign languages. Japanese schools were closed and the dissemination of information in the Japanese community was severely restricted.

1940s

World War II Impact

World War II significantly affected the Japanese community in Brazil.

The second generation of Japanese in Brazil gave up on returning to Japan, especially after the outbreak of the Second World War in 1941, which made it impossible to leave Brazil. During the war, community newspapers were closed down, and immigrants of German, Italian and Japanese origin were considered "Axis subjects" and seen as "enemies" after Brazil supported the US in 1942. At this time, repression against the Nikkei increased, with looting and destruction of Japanese property, and they were often provocatively referred to as the "fifth column".

Brazilian government took severe measures against the Japanese-Brazilians, including a ban on traveling without a pass, closing schools, seizing radios, and restricting the use of motor vehicles. Japanese companies were confiscated, including Banco América do Sul. Japanese immigrants faced more intense discrimination than Italians and Germans, with thousands being arrested or expelled on suspicion of espionage. Concentration camps were set up, and immigrants were forced to move, mainly in Santos, due to unfounded fears of espionage.

Post-War

Recovery and Integration

After World War II, the Japanese community began to recover and integrate into Brazilian society.

After the Second World War, Japanese immigrants in Brazil gave up returning to Japan due to the difficulties reported by relatives. Most families opted to prepare their children to rise in Brazilian society, resulting in a migration from the countryside to the cities. The strong anti-Japanese sentiment continued beyond the end of the war, with policies that favored European immigrants. In this context, Japanese-Brazilians began to get more involved in politics, electing Yukishige Tamura as a state representative in 1950 and, later, other representatives. Fábio Riodi Yassuda became the first Nikkei to be a minister in 1969.

In the 1950s, Japanese immigration began again, with almost 200,000 Japanese arriving in Brazil by 1973. With the migration to urban areas, many Japanese-Brazilians dedicated themselves to small businesses and basic services, such as dry cleaners. In the 1970s, 80% of the laundries in São Paulo were owned by Japanese. In addition, the education of their children became a priority, with many entering technical courses and prestigious colleges. By 1958, Japanese descendants accounted for 21% of Brazilians with higher education. The city of São Paulo became the city with the largest number of Japanese outside Japan, reflecting the integration and success of the Japanese-Brazilian community.

Street in Liberdade neighborhood, Sao Paulo, with Japanese streetlamps

1980s to today

A established and pendular community

From the end of the 1980s, an economic crisis in Brazil and Japanese demand for labor led around 85,000 Japanese and their descendants to emigrate to Japan, forming the dekassegui community. In 2008, approximately 300,000 Brazilians were living legally in Japan, mainly as industrial workers in cities such as Hamamatsu, Aichi, Shizuoka, Kanagawa, Saitama and Gunma. Brazil has more than 1.4 million people of Japanese origin, with São Paulo having the largest concentration, totaling 326,000 Japanese-Brazilians, according to the 1988 census.

Cultural aspects

Cusine

When the Japanese moved from the coffee plantations to the capital, they concentrated in the Liberdade district. There, Japanese eateries sprang up in independent basements. The Japanese introduced fruits and vegetables to Brazil that were unknown in local cuisine, cultivating previously infertile areas and changing Brazilian eating habits. Japanese cuisine gained popularity mainly in the 1980s in São Paulo, where a large Japanese community lives, but it was in the 1990s that Brazilians massively embraced this cuisine, appreciating its healthiness and taste. The expansion of rodízios and the presence of sushi in many establishments illustrate the growing demand, and in 2013 there were 600 Japanese restaurants in São Paulo compared to 500 steakhouses.

Japanese cuisine has won many fans in Brazil, and restaurants have adapted their dishes to suit the local palate, creating versions such as hot rolls. Free buffets or rodízios made Japanese cuisine more accessible, with the first restaurant of this type opening in 1990 in Rio de Janeiro. Fast-food models, such as temakerias, have also become popular, with the first temakeria opening in São Paulo in 2003. In Japan, there are no rodízios or temakerias, highlighting the Brazilian adaptations of Japanese cuisine.

Arts

In the arts, Japanese-Brazilian culture has stood out in cinema with films such as "Gaijin - Os Caminhos da Liberdade" , "Gaijin - Ama-me como Sou" , and "Corações Sujos" . In addition, various Japanese cultural events in Brazil, such as the Tanabata Matsuri in São Paulo, help to revive and promote age-old traditions, even among Brazilians of other origins. In the field of fine arts, Japanese-Brazilians created the "Japanese-Brazilian school", with groups like Seibi-kai, founded in 1935 in São Paulo, playing a crucial role in the evolution of the arts in Brazil. Seibi-kai organized 14 annual art salons between 1952 and 1970, contributing significantly to the national art scene until its closure in 1972. Another relevant group was the Guanabara Group, formed by Tikashi Fukushima and other Japanese-Brazilian and Italian artists, active between 1950 and 1960.

Ceramics also had notable contributions from Japanese-Brazilian artists such as Kimi Nii, internationally recognized and whose works were exhibited in Japan and at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) in New York. Hideko Honma became a national reference, standing out for her utilitarian ceramics, which were prized by Brazilian architects and chefs. In cinema, Tizuka Yamasaki, a Japanese-Brazilian filmmaker, founded her production company and directed award-winning films such as "Gaijin - Os Caminhos da Liberdade", receiving recognition in Brazil and abroad. In literature, Hidekazu Masuda Goga, an important diffuser of haiku, founded the Grêmio Haicai Ipê and was awarded for his work in spreading haiku internationally. On television, Japanese-Brazilian actors such as Daniele Suzuki face challenges of visibility and stereotypes, often being cast in caricatured roles that refer to their ethnic origin.

Sports

The Japanese influence in Brazil is also present in sport, especially through martial arts. Sports such as judo, karate, aikido and kendo were introduced by Japanese immigrants and became very popular. Judo, in particular, stood out with the formation of several academies and associations across the country, contributing to the development of high-level athletes who achieved international recognition. Brazilian athletes such as Aurélio Miguel and Rafaela Silva became Olympic champions, elevating judo to the status of one of Brazil's most successful sports in international competitions. Another striking example is jiu-jitsu, which originated in Japan and was adapted and transformed in Brazil, evolving into the famous Brazilian jiu-jitsu (BJJ), a distinctive style that has become a world reference. The practice of these martial arts not only promoted discipline and respect, fundamental values of Japanese culture, but also helped social integration and health promotion among Brazilians.

In addition to martial arts, baseball is another sport that Japanese immigrants brought to Brazil. Although less popular than soccer, baseball has found a significant niche, especially among the Japanese-Brazilian communities. Clubs and leagues were established, and cities like São Paulo and Bastos became centers of excellence for the sport. The Brazilian Baseball and Softball Confederation organizes championships and promotes participation in international competitions, showing the growing professionalization of the sport in the country. The practice of baseball has not only strengthened the cultural ties between Brazil and Japan, but has also contributed to the diversity of sports in the country, providing more options for young Brazilians to get involved in physical activities.
