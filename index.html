A walk through the history of Brazilian Japanese migration

History

Necessity for imigration in Japan

At the beginning of the 20th century, Japan faced overpopulation and agricultural difficulties after 265 years of isolation during the Edo period. The modernization and agrarian reform of the Meiji era, despite advances, caused unemployment and indebtedness among peasants, forcing many to migrate to already saturated cities. To ease social tensions and facilitate modernization, Japanese government encouraged emigration, mainly to the United States, Hawaii, Peru and Brazil. Minister Fukashi Sugimura's visit to Brazil in 1905, and his positive report on Brazil's receptiveness, intensified Japanese interest in Brazil, leading many to emigrate individually.

Necessity for imigration in Brazil

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Brazil's coffee economy demanded cheap labor, prompting a policy of immigration. The first official attempt to establish a diplomatic and commercial agreement with Japan took place in 1880, culminating in the signing of the Treaty of Friendship, Commerce and Navigation in 1895, which paved the way for Japanese immigration. Despite this, there was strong prejudice against Asian immigrants, seen as inferior races who would threaten the "whitening" of Brazil promoted by European immigration. Fears about the "yellow peril" and Japanese expansionism increased resistance, but the need for workers overcame these barriers. Ryo Mizuno, president of the Imperial Emigration Company, visited Brazil in 1906, and the contract to bring 3,000 Japanese immigrants was signed in 1907, despite criticism. The São Paulo government saw the Japanese as an alternative to fill the gap left by the Italians and solve labor conflicts, taking advantage of the Japanese workforce valued by positive reports of their performance in Hawaii.

Timeline of Events

1908: First Japanese Immigrants Arrive

The first Japanese immigrants arrived in Brazil on the Kasato Maru ship.

1920s: Growth of Japanese Communities

Japanese communities began to grow in São Paulo and other regions.

1930s: Restrictions and Challenges

During the 1930s, Japanese immigrants faced various restrictions and challenges.

1940s: World War II Impact

World War II significantly affected the Japanese community in Brazil.

Post-War: Recovery and Integration

After World War II, the Japanese community began to recover and integrate into Brazilian society.

Today: Legacy and Contributions

The Japanese-Brazilian community has made significant contributions to Brazilian culture and society.
